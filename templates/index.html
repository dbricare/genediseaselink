Examining genetic links to disease

A data-centric approach to drug development

Advantages

Measuring the strength of association between a medical condition and its genetic link will enable pharmaceutical companies to more efficiently develop new therapies.

  1. More accurate determination of the appropriate cellular pathway to target will improve the design and development of small molecule or biologic therapies that can counteract the effects of a disease.
  2. Provides researchers with human-validated targets and eliminates the need for traditional cell culture and animal model experiments, whose results aren't guaranteed to translate to humans.
  3. Laboratory-based drug discovery is very costly, time-consuming, and suffers from a low success rate.
  4. Gene-editing technologies such as CRISPR-Cas9 currently show strong promise as an entirely new treatment approach. If a disease-causing gene can be identified, it may be possible to develop a gene therapy to treat the genetic source directly.

Data collection

Data is contributed primarily by academic researchers who share their results through a variety of public databases.

  • A list of around 10,000 established genetic conditions is available from the NIH Genetics Home Reference.
  • A list of gene-disease associations from peer-reviewed journals and several public databases is available at DisGeNet. This database is current as of August 2015. This resource compiles information from:
    • Curated databases: UniProt, Comparative Toxicogenomics Database, ClinVar.
    • Predicted databases: Comparative Toxicogenomics Database, Mouse Genome Database, Rat Genome Database.
    • Literature resources: Genetic Association Database, Literature-derived Human Gene-Disease Network , BeFree.

Data analysis

The metric constructed here is based on work done for the DisGeNet database. The weighted score described below represents a metric [0,1] that describes the quality of the established link between the identified gene and associated diseases. Each gene may influence several diseases and each diseases may be influenced by several genes.

    Score = ( WUniProt + WCTDhuman + WClinVar ) + ( WRat + WMouse ) + ( WGAD + WLHGDN + WBeFree )
    Number of genes per disease (fewer gene targets indicate more well-defined and easier to treat disease pathology)

For the score calculation, each variable is either 0 or some positive value based on whether that gene-disease association appears in the corresponding database. The first term in parentheses represents curated databases, the second term is predicted databases and the third term takes into account the number of publications where the link appears. The DisGeNet About Page has more detailed information.
